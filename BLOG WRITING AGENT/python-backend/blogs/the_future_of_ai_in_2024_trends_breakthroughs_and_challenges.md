## Explore the Rise of Agentic AI and Smaller Models

Agentic AI represents a paradigm shift from passive, task-specific tools to autonomous systems capable of reasoning, planning, and executing multi-step workflows. Unlike traditional AI models that require explicit instructions for each action, agentic AI can break down complex problems—such as debugging code, analyzing datasets, or managing customer service interactions—into sequential tasks, often with minimal human oversight. This capability is particularly transformative in domains like software development, where agents can autonomously write, test, and refine code, or in enterprise workflows where they orchestrate multi-tool processes such as data extraction, transformation, and reporting. The potential for automation extends to real-time decision-making in logistics, healthcare diagnostics, and personalized education, where agentic systems adapt dynamically to new inputs and objectives.

Meta’s Llama family of models has emerged as a frontrunner in making sophisticated AI accessible beyond the cloud. Recent updates to Llama have delivered significant performance and efficiency gains: the models are now up to four times faster and 56% smaller than their predecessors, enabling deployment on edge devices such as smartphones and IoT platforms. These improvements reduce latency and bandwidth dependency, making AI inference feasible in low-power environments. For developers, this means the ability to embed advanced natural language understanding and generation directly into mobile apps without relying on cloud servers—ideal for privacy-sensitive or offline use cases.

## Analyze the Impact of AI in Disaster Forecasting and Climate Science

AI is transforming how we predict and respond to climate-related disasters, offering unprecedented accuracy and speed in forecasting extreme weather events. One standout innovation is **Google’s GenCast**, a generative AI model designed to enhance day-to-day weather predictions and extreme event forecasting. By leveraging advanced neural networks, GenCast provides more precise and timely warnings, enabling communities to prepare better for hurricanes, floods, and other natural disasters ([Source](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/products/2024-ai-extraordinary-progress-advancement/)).

Beyond weather forecasting, AI is revolutionizing climate modeling through projects like **NeuralGCM**, which simulates over 70,000 days of atmospheric conditions in the time it would take traditional physics-based models to simulate just 19 days. This dramatic speedup allows researchers to run more simulations, refine climate projections, and assess the impact of climate policies with greater agility ([Source](https://www.trendmicro.com/en_us/research/25/a/top-ai-trends-from-2024-review.html)). These advancements are not just theoretical—they are already being applied in real-world scenarios.

For instance, AI-driven forecasting is now integral to **hurricane tracking** and **wildfire prediction**, where early warnings can save lives and reduce economic losses. In 2024, AI models were deployed to predict the paths of major hurricanes with higher accuracy, giving emergency responders more time to evacuate residents and allocate resources ([Source](https://iot-analytics.com/ai-2024-10-most-notable-stories/)). Similarly, wildfire prediction models helped authorities identify high-risk areas, enabling proactive measures like controlled burns and firebreaks to prevent outbreaks.

The implications for **disaster preparedness, emergency response, and climate policy** are profound. More accurate forecasts allow governments and organizations to implement targeted interventions, from reinforcing infrastructure to deploying aid more efficiently. Policymakers are increasingly recognizing AI’s role in climate resilience, with initiatives like the **2024 update to the OECD AI Principles** emphasizing the need for responsible AI deployment in high-stakes domains like disaster management ([Source](https://digitalpolicyalert.org/ai-rules/2024-update-OECD-principles)).

However, these advancements also raise challenges. AI models require vast amounts of high-quality data, and biases in training datasets can lead to inaccurate predictions. Additionally, the computational resources needed to run these models at scale pose environmental concerns, highlighting the need for sustainable AI practices. As AI continues to evolve, its integration into climate science will depend on balancing innovation with accountability—ensuring that these tools are both powerful and equitable.

For technical professionals and policymakers, the takeaway is clear: AI is no longer a futuristic concept in climate science but a critical tool for mitigating the impacts of climate change. By investing in AI-driven forecasting, we can build more resilient systems, save lives, and create a more sustainable future.

## Navigate the Regulatory Landscape: EU AI Act and Global Policies

The regulatory environment for AI in 2024 has evolved rapidly, with landmark policies like the **EU AI Act** setting a global precedent. Adopted on **1 August 2024**, the EU AI Act introduces a **risk-based framework** that classifies AI systems into categories such as *unacceptable risk* (prohibited), *high risk*, *limited risk*, and *minimal risk*. High-risk systems—such as those used in critical infrastructure, law enforcement, or employment—face stringent requirements around transparency, data governance, and human oversight. Meanwhile, systems deemed *unacceptable risk*, including social scoring and certain types of predictive policing, are **banned outright**, with enforcement beginning **2 February 2025** [[1](https://www.mhc.ie/latest/insights/2024-in-review-key-legal-developments-in-ai)].

Across the Atlantic, the U.S. has taken a **principles-based approach**, focusing on mitigating risks like **discrimination, misinformation, and lack of transparency**—particularly salient in the lead-up to the **2024 U.S. election**. Agencies such as the FTC and NIST have emphasized accountability in AI deployment, encouraging voluntary frameworks while signaling potential regulatory action if harm occurs [[4](https://pluralpolicy.com/blog/ai-policy-2024/)].

Globally, the **OECD AI Principles were updated in 2024** to address emerging concerns. The revised principles now include provisions for **misinformation mitigation, environmental sustainability, security risks, and human agency**, reflecting a broader commitment to responsible AI development. These updates align with growing international consensus on balancing innovation with ethical safeguards [[5](https://digitalpolicyalert.org/ai-rules/2024-update-OECD-principles)].

For businesses, **compliance is no longer optional**. Companies must conduct **risk assessments**, document AI system design, and implement **governance mechanisms** to meet EU AI Act requirements. Proactive measures include:
- **Conducting AI impact assessments** for high-risk applications.
- **Establishing internal AI ethics boards** to review deployment.
- **Monitoring global regulatory updates**, especially in regions with emerging AI laws.

Failure to comply risks **fines, reputational damage, and operational disruptions**—as seen in several high-profile AI failures in 2024, where unchecked systems amplified bias or spread misinformation [[6](https://responsibleailabs.ai/knowledge-hub/articles/ai-safety-incidents-2024)][[7](https://www.technologyreview.com/2024/12/31/1109612/biggest-worst-ai-artificial-intelligence-flops-fails-2024/)].

The message is clear: **regulatory readiness is a competitive advantage**. Organizations that embed compliance into their AI development lifecycle—not as an afterthought, but as a core design principle—will not only avoid penalties but also build trust with users and regulators alike.

Regular third-party audits and adherence to emerging regulatory frameworks, like the 2024 OECD AI Principles update, can further reduce exposure to legal and operational liabilities [AI Rules: The 2024 update to the OECD AI Principles](https://digitalpolicyalert.org/ai-rules/2024-update-OECD-principles).

Ultimately, these incidents are a call to action. Organizations must foster a **culture of responsible AI**, where safety is prioritized from design to deployment. By learning from 2024’s missteps, the industry can build more trustworthy AI systems—ones that deliver value without compromising ethics or compliance.

## Evaluate the Environmental Impact of AI and Pathways to Sustainability

Large AI models are energy-intensive by design, with training runs for state-of-the-art systems consuming tens of megawatt-hours and inference adding ongoing operational load. These power demands translate directly into measurable carbon footprints, especially when powered by grids still reliant on fossil fuels. The resulting environmental pressure has pushed sustainability to the forefront of AI governance, culminating in the 2024 update to the OECD AI Principles that explicitly calls on developers and deployers to assess and mitigate environmental impacts throughout the AI lifecycle.

Industry and academia are already responding. Organizations are pairing renewable-powered data centers with algorithmic innovations such as sparse attention mechanisms and quantization to cut compute requirements by double-digit percentages without sacrificing accuracy. Carbon-aware scheduling—running workloads when local grids are richest in renewables—has moved from pilot projects to production in several hyperscale clouds, demonstrating that operational changes can yield immediate reductions in AI’s carbon intensity.

For businesses, the path to greener AI starts with measurement. Adopting standardized sustainability metrics—such as energy per inference, carbon per training run, and PUE-adjusted facility emissions—enables apples-to-apples tracking and transparent reporting. Architectural optimizations like model distillation and pruning should be paired with infrastructure choices (e.g., colocation near renewable generation) and lifecycle policies that retire or downsize models once their marginal utility declines. Embedding these practices into procurement checklists and engineering OKRs institutionalizes accountability and turns sustainability from a side project into a core deliverable.
